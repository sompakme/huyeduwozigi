How Ellen DeGeneres Lost Her Annual Salary After Her Show Ended Amid Scandal

Ellen DeGeneres's journey to becoming a multi-millionaire in show business started with her 1990s sitcom, "Ellen." However, venturing into daytime TV was the real game changer that pegged her annual salary between $75 million to $90 million. The Ellen DeGeneres Show, named after the award-winning host, was a ray of sunshine among millions of fans. The syndicated talk show included fun segments like celebrity chat sessions that were sometimes controversial, pranks, dance breaks and heart-melting humanitarian causes. All of these were anchored on DeGeneres's famous mantra, "Be Kind."

However, in a dramatic turn of events, the fun atmosphere appeared to be a smokescreen after insiders who worked on the set of the show revealed their undesirable experiences. What happened to The Ellen Show? There were claims of hostility and harassment with Ellen DeGeneres and three major producers at the center of it all. There were also claims that the TV star showed her true colors during commercial breaks. These revelations tainted the wholesome content DeGeneres displayed before she bowed out of TV two years later.

According to The Richest, by the time Ellen DeGeneres ended her show, she earned $50 million annually, and she parted ways with the hefty sum, albeit facing backlash for her workplace issues. DeGeneres was primarily known for her eponymous show, but it was not her sole source of income. The TV star's career portfolio extends to voice acting, film, entrepreneurship and a judging stint on "American Idol."

Inside The Toxic Workplace Accusations Against The Ellen DeGeneres Show

In July 2020, former and current employees of The Ellen DeGeneres Show told Buzzfeed News about their unpleasant experiences on set. Some former staffers stated that they lost their jobs by taking medical leave or bereavement days. Others revealed that their managers instructed them not to speak with Ellen DeGeneres if they saw her on the premises, thereby painting her as unapproachable. One insider told the publication:

"That 'be kind' bullsh*t only happens when the cameras are on. It's all for the show. I know they give money to people and help them out, but it's for the show."

Most of those who shared their grievances stated that the executive producers and other senior managers were the major catalysts of toxicity. However, one person insisted that Ellen DeGeneres needed to "take more responsibility" because "it's Ellen's name on the show." The insider added that the TV star was often surrounded by managers who convinced her that "things are going great."
